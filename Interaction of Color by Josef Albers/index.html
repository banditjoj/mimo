Interaction of Color by Josef Albers

  • Figure - Groud Relationship: Perception of whether colour is on top or bottom

  • Relativity: Colour perceived differently depending on the background colour

  • Relationship: 2 different colours perceived the same by maintaining the relationship, e.g. 3 reds placed onto white background will appear similar / same, but put next to each other it’s evident that they are all different reds

  • Bezold Effect: colours optically mix when viewed from a distance, changing one colour will chnage the relationships of all the other colours, same colour on a lighter or darker background affects the perception from optical mixing
  • Colour Intervals: Like C chord and A chord are different chords but with the same intervals, colours can also be seen in different “chords” with the same intervals
  • Weber-Frechner Law: people are more sensitive to changes in colour in light versus in shadow. For example, below, it is easier to distinguise betweten 10 and 20 versus 110 and 120 despite the same difference of the two. Because of the logarithmic perception of colour, shadow needs to be stepped up much darker in order to create the perception of even shadow. e.g. difference between 0 sugars and 1 sugars in a coffee vs 8 sugars and 9 sugars in a coffee. The greater the stimilus, the more of it you need to detect a change. If you start a painting darker, you will need to step darker down further to create a difference in darkness, and vice versa with light. The perceptual difference between 20% and 30% grey is larger versus 80% and 90% grey.

  • Warm - Cool Relationship: Rather than being an absolute, it’s relative, for example, a warm blue can look warmer than a cool red

  • Boundaries: 2 similar high contrast colours will create a vibrating boundary, whereas 2 similar low contrast colours will have a blurred boundary
